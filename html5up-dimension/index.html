Projects

For my project, my goal was to design a light that is able to turn on and off via sound. The utility of this centers around solving the inconviences of having to manually reach for the on button or a remote in order to turn on and change the color of lights.

The first step of this process was determining what materials I would need in order to make this work. After some research on similar projects that have been completed online, I determined that I would need an Arduino or similar microcontroller, a SparkFun sound detector or similar audio sensor, a breadboard + jumper wires, an LED strip, a 9V battery, a 9V battery connector, an audio splitter, a resistor, and an NPN transistor.

Next, I set up the circuit on the breadboard. I connected the positive end of the LED to the 9V power supply through the 330-ohm resistor, and the negative end to the ground. I added the NPN transistor to control when the LED turned on and off.

After that, I wired the sound detector to the arduino and the RGB LED. The sound detector allows the LED to react to sound and sound intensity. This could then be customized based on what I wanted in the programming stage.

I decided that I wanted to control the LED color based on the intensity of the sound. I used code from the SparkFun website as a starting point and modified it as needed. This part took the longest as the data from the microphone wasn't clean or consistent so it was hard to get a reliable color. To address this, I spent a few days learning about microphone algorithms that toned down static noise and random interference issues.

Now that I had the internals functional to an extent, I began work on designing the enclosure. Using a combination of blender and solidworks, I was able to design a light saber that hid the components within the handle and lined the inside of the saber portion with the LEDs. I started with a test print of the handle to ensure that the components I was using would fit inside.

Fortunately, double checking on the dimensions whilst CADing paid off and everything fit first try. The handle did end up being a little chunkier than I was expecting but it was nothing that would stop this project from being successful. Now that I had finished with the handle and placing the components inside, I got to work printing a draft of the saber portion itself. The sabers are a series of tubes that are slightly wider at the bottom so that when pushed through each other, they stop before coming out fully.

Once everything was assembled, I tested out the sound activation. I intended for the saber sliding out of the handle to generate enough sound to activate the lights which would be a cool transition. Unfortunately, the algorithm that I used to limit static sounds from interfering with the microphone imputs were negating the sliding sound from passing the needed threshhold to activate the light. The light can still be activated by smacking the handle but even loud external noise sometimes doesn't trigger the light because the microphone sits within the handle which is solid. I plan to fix this by drilling a small hole for the microphone to be exposed to the raw sounds of its environment.

With that being said, a smack on the handle to activate the light isn't half bad and the light saber did turn out pretty well visually. I had some fun testing the light penetration of sabers with different colored filament. Check out some more photos of it featured below.
